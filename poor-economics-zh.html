The Economics of the S-Curve: Thoughts on Poor Economics by Abhijit Banerjee and Esther Duflo | 读后感：穷人的S曲线经济学

by Iris - Wed, 22 Oct 2025
Tags: #Snippets

This is a belated reading note.

I finished reading the book Poor Economics: A Radical Rethinking of the Way to Fight Global Poverty by Abhijit Banerjee and Esther Duflo during the Chinese New Year (in February 2025). The whole book took me about half a year to mull over, bit by bit through pieces of slack slots (mostly on my way to work on trams). Yet, the key takeaways leave a lasting impression that provokes deep thoughts on my own life as a normal person who do face lots of constraints (versus the super-rich who at least do not have to worry about money or can use money to resolve their constraints).

1. Heavy push, both monetary and mental, is needed for the poor to exit traps.

The authors propose that the relationship between income today and income in the future is an S-shaped curve – flat at the beginning and then rising rapidly before flatting again. The S-shape means that for the poor at the flat end of the curve, if they are not getting large enough income today, they can only be poorer tomorrow (as income tomorrow will be less than today). Only those who can afford to invest enough to reach the inflection point are able to grow income or wealth at a very fast rate. Even though there are microfinance programs or fundings available to help the poor, the amount is usually NOT large enough to push the poor out of the trap.

On the other hand, the authors note ideology, ignorance, and inertia (the 3 Is) are the enforcing factors for the poverty trap, aside from lack of investment. Some poor people do not believe they can cross the hump, thus staying within the trap passively (ideology and inertia). Or they are too risk-averse to invest enough, then become pessimistic after a few failures and never invest again (ideology). Or they are mentally constrained with the many difficulties in life so unable to think from the long term and make the right choice (ignorance).

Aside from financial aids, it does require a mental push to boost confidence, knowledge and effort of the poor to get out of the trap. For example, long-term goals may look too far to be achievable, so short-term goals should be set to keep motivating the poor with interim outcomes. The tools to combat poverty already exit but are not always known to the poor, hence education is needed. Creating opportunities for the poor to shine is also important as it inspires people to mobilize the fundings available and drives them to invest whatever they have for their way out of poverty.

2. The same 3 Is (ideology, ignorance, and inertia) apply to normal people.

We are facing the same problem of the lack of self-control, short-sightedness, lack of confidence, procrastination, etc. as the poor. As human beings, we share the same problems. Therefore, when studying the solutions to poverty, the authors suggest that we cannot treat “the poor” as abnormal people. Instead, we imagine as if we are facing the same real world constraints as the poor, ponder the natural human reactions, and design mechanisms to influence the behavior of the poor.

3. Empathy is the key to understand and resolve issues.

The authors do not assume that the poor’s way of living and thinking is inferior or of no value. Hence, they are able to take time and effort to really empathize with the poor to uncover the underlying logic of their lives, "in all their complexity and richness," to come at a theory on the cause of poverty, then solutions to it. Probably, the appropriate mindset to study the problem of the poor is that

since we are not the poor, we cannot easily judge them unless we fully understand their issues from their perspective.

In fact, the same empathetic approach applies to all cases where significant status/identity gaps exist. Too often, people in an advantageous position, either in terms of wealth, title or age, look down on other people inferior to them and superimpose their own assumptions that may not always be correct. Such bias will never lead to true understanding of the issues on hand, let alone solutions.

今年过年的时候读完了《穷人的经济学》，前两天突然翻出半篇2月份没写完的读后感，打补丁式地补全了小作文。

这本书断断续续读了半年，大多是在上班的叮叮车上看的。所以细节一概没记住，印象最深刻的就是两位作者研究“穷人”这一人群的人文态度-将心比心。他们不把“穷人”当成异类，而是站在他们的角度思考问题，通过一个一个随机试验、访谈验证想法，剖析穷人行为背后的逻辑。他们不会站在社会地位的制高点上去评价、批判、说教穷人的生活，而是真正地去“理解”他们，找到痛点，对症下药。

一个主要的理论是S曲线，即今天的收入和未来收入的关系是S形的。一开始曲线平缓，明天的收入（财富）没有今天的多，只有收入（财富）达到一定水平之后，才能实现财富增值。如果投资不足（投资后的财富没有达到增值区），穷人再怎么努力还是没钱。

因此，要帮助穷人，我们需要大方给钱。但是光给钱还不够。穷人的心态也很重要，直接决定他们拿到钱之后如何执行脱贫计划。作者提出三个I（意识形态/心态ideology，无知ignorance，惯性inertia）来解释脱贫失败的原因。

  • 意识形态/心态：穷人受困于一些落后的思想，或是不相信他们可以脱贫；
  • 无知：穷人不知道自己可以获得的帮助有哪些，或穷人因为要对付生活中的很多问题，没有精力去考虑长远，做出最优选择；
  • 惯性：穷人不敢做出行动，害怕承担风险；拖延症；缺乏自控力

据此，脱贫的方法有：教育；给（穷）人认识自己价值的机会（创业）；设置短期可实现的目标，保持（穷）人的积极性。这些方法去掉“穷”字其实也可以看作是解决一般就业问题的思路。
